## The problem

USDA Farm Service Agency disaster-assistance programs — the **Livestock Forage Disaster Program (LFP)**, **Emergency Assistance for Livestock, Honeybees, and Farm-Raised Fish (ELAP)**, and the **Noninsured Crop Disaster Assistance Program (NAP)** — make billions of dollars in payments to American producers each year. Eligibility for those payments hinges on two key inputs:

1. **The Normal Grazing Period (NGP)** assigned to each county, which defines the window during which livestock are presumed to be on various types of pasture.
2. **The US Drought Monitor (USDM)**, which provides the drought-severity classification that triggers fast-track disaster designations and program eligibility.

Both inputs were established years ago and have not been systematically reassessed against the climate data and on-the-ground conditions that govern grazing today. The result is that some counties qualify for relief that no longer reflects their growing season, while others fall through the cracks. FSA county staff — the people most directly responsible for delivering these programs — have flagged these mismatches for years.

## What this project does

This is a three-year research partnership between the [Montana Climate Office](https://climate.umt.edu) at the University of Montana and the USDA Office of the Chief Economist, the USDA Climate Hubs, and the Farm Service Agency. The work has produced four complementary outputs, all openly available on this site:

- **Re-grounded the science.** Using the official **NAP-190** guidance and the latest **NOAA nClimGrid** climatology, the team rebuilt the county-level Normal Grazing Period calculation as a fully reproducible workflow. The same pipeline can be re-run on each new climate normal — so program criteria can stay current with measured conditions rather than going stale. The full report is published on the [Analysis page](/analysis/).
- **Closed data gaps.** Through Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) requests, the team obtained, cleaned, and publicly archived the official county-level Normal Grazing Periods, LFP eligibility determinations, the LFP county boundaries used in those determinations, and the FSA county boundary geodatabases. Alongside the FOIA archives, the project maintains weekly mirrors of the US Drought Monitor (January 2000 – present) and the Secretarial and Presidential disaster designations that trigger fast-track assistance. The full set of eleven archives is surfaced on the [Data page](/data/).
- **Listened to FSA staff and producers.** Graduate research conducted with national FSA approval has produced **four completed M.S. theses** examining how the LFP and related programs are delivered on the ground — what works, what doesn't, and where program design and producer experience diverge. Two additional theses advised by the PI extend that work to drought planning in the Northern Great Plains and rangeland restoration in western Montana. The four theses are listed alongside the project's analytical reports on the [Analysis page](/analysis/); the [Team page](/team/) names the researchers and their faculty committee chairs.
- **Anticipated future shifts.** Using NASA's downscaled CMIP6 projections (NEX-GDDP), the team has characterized how grazing periods are likely to shift under future climate conditions; a peer-reviewed manuscript reporting these findings is in preparation.

In the course of this work the team identified a notable program-administration finding: LFP eligibility has been determined using a non-authoritative county boundary dataset. The finding was presented to USDA OCE-OEEP; a fuller report is in preparation.

## Why it matters

The aim is simple: **disaster relief programs should be responsive, timely, and grounded in operational reality.** When the data behind eligibility decisions are transparent, current, and publicly auditable, USDA can:

- Avoid misallocations and improve the **fiscal integrity** of emergency-assistance spending.
- **Reduce red tape** for FSA county staff by giving them better tools and authoritative reference data.
- Deliver **effective, targeted relief** to ranchers when and where it is actually needed.
- Base program criteria on **transparent, science-based metrics** that any researcher, producer, or oversight body can independently reproduce.

## Looking ahead

The remaining months of the project translate these results into briefings, factsheets, and a national working meeting with USDA OCE and FSA staff aimed at concrete program-administration improvements. Findings will also be shared with FSA state offices through regional webinars hosted by the USDA Climate Hubs.

## Scientific foundation

The project builds on peer-reviewed work demonstrating that drought-assessment methods have not kept pace with the climate record — most directly, Hoylman, Bocinsky, and Jencso (2022), *"Drought assessment has been outpaced by climate change: empirical arguments for a paradigm shift,"* in [*Nature Communications*](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-022-30316-5). That paper provides the empirical case for re-anchoring drought thresholds to current climate normals; the present project translates that case into reproducible workflows and authoritative data archives for FSA program administration.
